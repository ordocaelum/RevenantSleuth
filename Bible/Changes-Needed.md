**Continuity Notes, Inconsistencies, and Open Questions for the Author**

## A. Kane's First Name — "Adrian" Is Never Stated On-Page Through Ch.6

**Location in Character Bible:**
> "POV: Detective **Adrian Kane**, first-person."
> Section 1: "Detective ADRIAN KANE — Protagonist"

**Location in The Story (Ch.1–6):**
Kane is referred to only as "Detective Kane" (by DeMarco, by the sergeant, by Grant), "Kane" (by Evelyn/Sinclair), or in first-person narration. His first name "Adrian" does not appear anywhere in the prose through the end of Ch.6.

**Is this a problem?**
Not necessarily — noir protagonists are often first-name-absent for long stretches (Marlowe, Spade). But the Character Bible treats "Adrian" as canon, and the video adaptation should have at least one organic moment to establish it. If the book never voices it, the adaptation has no hook for it.

**Proposed fix options (author's choice):**
1. *Seed it early via Max:* In Ch.6 or Ch.7, when Max makes direct contact, he says "Adrian" — slowly, deliberately, as the Character Bible's signature tell describes. This is the most emotionally loaded placement. It would signal intimacy and threat simultaneously.
2. *Seed it via Grant:* In the suspension hearing or a private conversation, Grant uses his first name once — the way authority uses first names when they're serious. "Adrian. Sit down."
3. *Leave it until Ch.11:* Evelyn uses it for the first time at the moment of earned trust/confrontation. Most restrained option.

**Recommendation:** Option 1 (Max says "Adrian" in Ch.7). It fits the Character Bible's stated tell exactly and turns the name introduction into a horror beat.

## B. Evelyn → Sinclair Naming Shift Between Ch.4 and Ch.5

**In the prose:**
- Ch.1–4: Kane's narration uses "Evelyn" when referring to her: *"She was at the far end of the bar. Evelyn."* / *"'Objective is information,' I said."* / *"I found Evelyn in the red-lamp room."*
- Ch.5 onward: The narration switches to "Sinclair": *"Evelyn Sinclair sat on the edge of the bed"* (introductory full name), then *"Sinclair's eyes narrowed"* / *"Sinclair watched my hands"* throughout Ch.5 and Ch.6.

**The question:** Is this intentional POV distancing, or an authorial naming inconsistency?

**The argument for intentional:**
The Character Bible explicitly describes Kane as using "last names to distance; first names when it matters." After Ch.4 — after she was captured, exposed, and they fought their way out together — Kane would rationally rebuild his professional armor. He *cares* about her now, which is dangerous. Calling her "Sinclair" is how he holds that at arm's length. This reading is fully consistent with the bible.

**The argument for inconsistency:**
There's no on-page beat where Kane explicitly makes this shift — no narrative note, no dialogue, no internal observation. It happens between chapters without acknowledgment. This could read as authorial drift rather than character choice.

**Proposed fix:**
Add one line at the start of Ch.5, in Kane's internal voice, that acknowledges the shift — even obliquely. Something like: *"I stopped thinking of her as Evelyn somewhere between the basement and the motel. Sinclair was easier. Sinclair had edges."* This converts authorial drift into a deliberate character beat and gives the eventual return to "Evelyn" (if and when it comes) its earned weight.

## C. The Story Ends Mid-Ch.6 — What Chapters Are Missing

**From the Character Bible:**
> The bible explicitly covers Chapters 1–11 with character arcs, knowledge flags, and relationship tensions mapped out to the end of Book 1.

**From The Story:**
> Only Chapters 1–6 exist, and Ch.6 is labeled "(Rough Draft)" and ends at the waterfront dock scene without resolution. The runner steps off the pier. Kane runs toward the watcher in the fog. The watcher disappears. Last line: *"And wherever it is… they're reporting to someone higher."*

**Missing material (Ch.7–11):**
- Ch.7: Max's first direct contact; K4 crystallization; Grant's political deadline; Evelyn's bass sensitivity worsening.
- Ch.8: War footing; Rook introduced; departmental suspension hearing; Max's next psychological move.
- Ch.9: Pattern clarity; Evelyn's witness backstory fully told; K4 publicly confirmed; Voss's hand forced; Rook near-claimed.
- Ch.10: Citywide bass architecture revealed; Max partially claimed; Voss discarded; mirror reveal moment at chapter end (earliest K5 option).
- Ch.11: Final confrontation; K5 full reveal; Max's fate; Lich contained (not destroyed); Kane vindicated; epilogue bruise.

**Open questions for the author:**
1. What exactly is the "Bridge" incident? The Polaroid note says "you still grab sleeves" — implying Kane grabbed Max's sleeve (or someone else's) on a bridge. Did someone die? Did Kane try to stop it and fail? Did Max order him to let go? The story gives the weight of the memory without the specifics. Readers will want this answered in Ch.7 or Ch.8.
2. What is in the Bridge file that was stolen? What leverage does it give Max?
3. Who is the figure in the fog at the dock? A named character, or an unnamed Lich-proxy?
4. What is Rook Alvarez's entry point? The Character Bible flags him as a potential near-victim — how does he intersect with Kane's existing case?
5. What does "partially claimed" look like for Max in Ch.10–11? Is it visual (the cold; the branching veins)? Is it behavioral (full archaic register)? Does he fight it or embrace it?

## D. Vial Count and Continuity Notes

**From Ch.4:**
> Kane sees a "fridge, locked: rows of vials bleeding cold through the seals." He takes **one vial** from the basement. Later (Ch.5 morgue) he also produces "a smaller tube from my coat — my backup." So he left the club with *at least two samples*.

**Potential continuity issue:**
Ch.5: Cho tells Kane *"don't keep any of it on you"* and Kane hands over the backup tube. The main vial is locked in Cho's fridge. But Ch.6 doesn't explicitly confirm Kane is now vial-free. If a future chapter needs Kane to have the vial on him, this chain needs to be verified.

**Proposed tracking note:**
Establish a clear "vial custody" chain:
- After Ch.5 morgue: Cho holds the main vial (locked, coded); Kane gave him the backup. **Kane has no vial.**
- The dock runner's cooler case (Ch.6) suggests there is *new* potential for Kane to acquire another sample in Ch.7+.

## F. The Stitched-Mouth Attacker in Ch.4 — Continuity of Horror

**From Ch.4:**
> An attacker with a stitched mouth — body "wrong-angled," moving like something assembled by someone who'd heard about anatomy secondhand. Kane fights it with improvised weapons. It's not dead. It doesn't die the way things should.

**Issue:**
The attacker is fought off but the narrative doesn't explicitly confirm it was destroyed, neutralized, or escaped. When Kane and Evelyn fight their way out, the attacker's fate is not stated. This is potentially intentional (horror-ambiguity), but if the attacker reappears in Ch.7+, its continuity status needs to be established.

**Proposed fix:**
Either (a) add a line in the exit fight that confirms the attacker was at least temporarily incapacitated (fell, stopped moving), or (b) deliberately leave it open as a recurring threat. If the latter, set a note here: the stitched-mouth figure from the Ch.4 basement may be one of several identical units and should be treated as an archetype, not a unique character.

## H. Voss "Letting Kane Go" — The Missing Beat

**From Ch.3:**
> Voss seizes Kane's comms and speaks with him directly. The implication is that Voss had Kane in a vulnerable position (rooftop, no backup, Evelyn captured) and chose not to escalate.

**From the Character Bible:**
> "Lets Kane go against his preference (reader never told why)."

**Issue:**
The reason Voss allows Kane to proceed (or doesn't order him taken/killed) is never explained and should never be explained before Ch.10–11 (it's Lich-level override — the Lich wants Kane alive and investigating because Kane is being guided toward something). This is correct reveal discipline. However, the video adaptation should make Voss's restraint *feel* strange — visually strange, behaviorally strange — without explaining it. A note in every storyboard involving Voss: his reluctance should read as a man who started to give an order and then *didn't*, as if something overruled him.

## I. Cho's Charm — Diegetic or Metaphorical?

**From Ch.6 prologue:**
> Cho keeps "a worn charm on a chain his grandmother had insisted would 'keep the bad air away.'" The charm falls during the vial's whisper-episode.

**Issue:**
Is the charm's fall a supernatural event (the Lich actively knocking it down) or a coincidence that Cho's stressed mind over-interprets? The prose keeps it ambiguous intentionally. However, the video adaptation must make a production decision about whether to *show* it moving (suggesting supernatural agency) or simply show Cho hearing whispers and noticing it fell (diegetic ambiguity). Recommendation: show the charm move *very slightly* — one millimeter, barely perceptible on screen — then fall. Keep the ambiguity. Do not render supernatural force explicitly.
