# REVENANT SLEUTH — Style Guide
**Series Tone Bible · Noir/Urban Horror for Unreal Engine / Blender YouTube Production**
*This guide governs all visual, audio, and narrative choices for the adaptation. It is the law of the series — not a suggestion.*

---

## Core Identity Statement
REVENANT SLEUTH is a **noir detective story that became a horror story**, and the horror works because the detective voice never breaks. It is told in the first person, past tense, by a man who survived it — which means the viewer always has the implicit understanding that Kane makes it out. The horror is not "will he live" but "what does living cost him."

The Max Payne DNA runs throughout: internal monologue as confessional, violence as punctuation not spectacle, the city as a character with its own corruption and weather. The occult escalation earns its weight because the detective procedural was doing real work first.

---

## 1. VOICEOVER RULES

### Kane's Internal Monologue (VO)
- **Tense:** Past tense throughout. This is retrospective testimony.
- **Register:** Hardboiled, sardonic, image-dense. Short sentences. No wasted words. Metaphors that land like punches.
- **Rhythm:** Alternates between clipped observations (2–5 words) and longer introspective sentences. Never monotone. The punctuation is emotional.
- **Example style reference from the source:**
  > *"December makes the city honest. Not in a good way. In a stripped-down-to-the-bone way — when the wind gets mean and the streets stop pretending they're not hungry."*
- **Key rule:** The VO comments on what the camera is showing, but it is not redundant. If we can see Kane's hands are busy, the VO does not say "my hands were busy." It says something the camera cannot show — the feeling, the history, the dread.
- **VO and horror:** Kane never screams in voiceover. When things get frightening, his voice gets quieter, more controlled. The tighter the control, the worse the situation.

### When NOT to Use VO
- In action sequences (Ch.4 basement fight, Ch.6 dock run): silence the VO during physical beats. Let the environment and the audio do the work. The VO returns after the action as processing, not narration.
- In dialogue-heavy scenes: VO may introduce and close, but the conversation carries the scene. Don't narrate over a conversation.
- The mirror room (Ch.4): **no VO during the mirror beat.** The shape in the reflection should arrive in silence. The VO returns after Kane and Evelyn have left the room.

## 2. COLOR SCRIPT

### The Emotional Palette
The series uses color as emotional notation. These are not arbitrary choices — they map directly to Kane's psychological state and the level of occult contamination in a scene.

| Scene Type | Dominant Color | Secondary | What It Communicates |
|---|---|---|---|
| Street / city establishing shots | **December grey** — desaturated blue-grey | Sodium amber from street lamps | The honest city: cold, tired, non-judgmental |
| Pre-contamination scenes | Warm amber (bar, apartment) | Grey shadows | Human warmth in a cold world |
| The Velvet Abyss — exterior | **Neon purple** dominant | Blood red accents | Predatory seduction; the wound |
| The Velvet Abyss — interior | Purple + amber (warm strobe) | Deep shadow pools | Controlled seduction; hidden architecture |
| Red velvet corridor | Warm amber (spare) + deep red | Gold trim | Aristocratic menace; the system's face |
| Basement factory | **Clinical white** (fluorescent) | Cold blue at fridge | Horror through clarity: the machine laid bare |
| Motel | **Sickly yellow** throughout | No relief color | Guilt, temporary, no sanctuary |
| Morgue | Clinical white | Cold blue (vial/fridge presence) | Science vs. the irrational |
| Contaminated persons / vial effects | **Cold blue** bleed into scene | No warmth | The cold that doesn't belong |
| Kane's wound | Bandage white → bruise purple → cold blue tint | Pale skin | The invasion becoming personal |
| Projected Ch.10–11 | Grey and cold dominating; warmth shrinking | Deep red only where violence lives | The architecture taking hold |

### The Monitor Green
- Body-cam footage, computer screens, security monitors: a **sickly green-tinted CRT/monitor aesthetic**. This is distinct from the main palette and marks "mediated truth" — the horror seen through a screen rather than experienced directly.
- This color also appears in the bullpen at the opening of Ch.1: "that glow painted everyone's faces the same sick color — greenish, hollow, like the room had been underwater for too long."

### Color Temperature Gradient
- **Warmth = humanity / uncontaminated.** When Kane is safe (relatively), the color is amber, warm.
- **Cold = contamination / Lich presence.** When the vial, the markings, or the Lich's indirect influence are nearby, the color temperature drops.
- The series tracks Kane's contamination by the subtle **creep of blue-cold into the warm scenes** — by Ch.6, even his apartment has a slight blue tint when he rolls up his sleeve.

## 3. LIGHTING LANGUAGE

### Core Rule: One Key, One Fill, One Practical
Low-poly production means we cannot have complex multi-light rigs. Every scene uses:
- **One key light** (the primary source — where it comes from and what it says)
- **One fill** (low intensity, opposing direction, prevent total blackout in shadow areas)
- **One or two practicals** (diegetic lights: lamps, neon signs, monitors, candles — these are the emotional lights)

The practicals do the storytelling. The technical lights just keep the scene readable.

### Scene-by-Scene Lighting Signatures
**Outdoor City (Night):**
- Key: overhead sodium street lamp (amber, slightly orange)
- Fill: ambient city sky (near-black blue)
- Practical: neon signs at distance (purple/red bleed into frame edges)
- Character light: the practical neon should hit one side of Kane's face when facing the club; pure shadow on the other side

**The Velvet Abyss Interior:**
- Key: colored overhead wash (purple)
- Fill: floor reflection (purple + dark)
- Practical: bar backlit bottles (amber) + strobes (white flash every 3rd beat)
- The strobe is the only white light in the scene — it reveals the room for one frame before darkness returns

**Basement Factory:**
- Key: overhead fluorescent (cool white, flat, institutional)
- Fill: secondary fluorescent at lower intensity (same temperature)
- Practical: the fridge seal (cold blue) — the only light that matters narratively
- The flat even light here is *deliberate* — this is the least atmospheric space in the series, which makes it the most frightening

**Kane's Apartment:**
- Key: desk lamp (warm amber, tight falloff)
- Fill: window ambient (city glow, very low)
- Practical: the flashlight (Ch.1) — a hard white moving key during the discovery scene
- The flashlight should cast moving shadows that give the room a shape before the desk lamp is established

**Motel:**
- Key: nightstand lamp (yellow-warm)
- Fill: window bleed (parking-lot sodium, same yellow)
- No cool light anywhere — the sickly yellow is total

**Morgue:**
- Key: overhead fluorescents (flat cool white — no shadows)
- The flatness is the point: in the morgue, nothing hides
- Exception: when the vial is out, add a very slight blue point light at the tray (very low intensity, tiny radius). Just enough to shift the temperature near the vial

## 4. LENS LANGUAGE / CAMERA VOCABULARY

### General Rules
- **Low angle, slightly below eye-level** for Kane in authority-establishing shots. He is physically imposing even when the city dwarfs him.
- **Eye-level for dialogue** — the camera is in the conversation, not observing it.
- **High angle for contaminated persons** — the kneeling user in traffic, the dock runner — to suggest diminishment, smallness, the loss of standing that comes with compliance.
- **Very slow push-in for revelation shots** — the discovery of DeMarco's head, the vial's centrifuge rejoin, the shape in the mirror. The camera does not cut. It approaches.
- **Static holds for Lich-adjacent content** — the mirror shape, the basement fridge, the figure in the fog. The patience of the Lich is expressed in camera stillness. The camera does not rush toward what it doesn't understand.

### Shot Grammar Per Scene Type
**Action sequences (Ch.4 alley, Ch.4 basement fight, Ch.6 dock):**
- Cuts are faster — 1.5 to 2.5 seconds per shot
- Camera is closer to the subject — tighter framing, less context
- But: when the bass obedience activates, **the cut rhythm slows**. The command brings stillness to the edit

**Investigation / dialogue:**
- Cuts are slower — 4 to 8 seconds per shot
- Two-shot coverage with eyeline matching
- Reaction shots are held longer than feels comfortable — the pause is part of the conversation

**Horror revelation:**
- No cut. The camera slowly discovers.
- If there must be a cut, it is to a reaction shot (Kane's face, Evelyn's face) before returning to the subject
- Never cut away from the mirror shape at the moment it's noticed. Hold

### The "Pocket Check" Convention
Whenever Kane reaches for or touches the vial (in his pocket), the camera catches a close-up of his hand against the coat. This is recurring and becomes a visual vocabulary item — the audience learns that this gesture means "the vial is present and draining him."

## 5. PACING AND RHYTHM

### Episode Structure
Each episode should follow a rough emotional arc:
1. **Open on tension** — do not ease the viewer in. Start on the ongoing problem.
2. **Investigation middle** — the slowest section; the audience breathes; details accumulate.
3. **Revelation / escalation** — the scene that advances the horror or the knowledge flags.
4. **Cliffhanger or unresolved threat** — end on an open question, not a closed one.

### The VO as Metronome
The voiceover pacing sets the emotional rhythm of the episode:
- Short, clipped VO = tension, movement, something is about to happen
- Long, reflective VO = aftermath, processing, the moment when Kane sits with what he knows
- **No VO = the worst moments.** The silences are the crisis points.

### December Cold as Pacing Tool
Breath fog, hunched shoulders, the physical discomfort of December — these are pacing tools. When the production budget is tight, an additional breath-particle pass on a character signals that time is passing and the cold is relentless. It's free continuity.

## 6. ADAPTED DIALOGUE RULES

### Consistent, Not Exact
The source novel's dialogue is not a script. It is a score. The adaptation may:
- Compress long exchanges into shorter exchanges (cut lines that don't advance the scene)
- Reorder lines for visual blocking (move a line from "at the bar" to "in the doorway" if the scene plays better that way)
- Paraphrase while preserving the meaning and voice (the noir register must remain; the vocabulary may shift slightly)

### Never:
- Contradict the emotional content of a scene
- Remove a K-flag reveal from a scene (if a scene contains K2 confirmation, the adapted dialogue must still confirm K2)
- Make the Lich truth explicit before Ch.10–11
- Give Kane optimism he doesn't earn
- Make Evelyn/Sinclair sentimental before her arc reaches it

### The Voice Test
Read the adapted dialogue aloud. If it could not plausibly have been written by the same person who wrote: *"I watch. I listen. I bury what I can't fix."* — revise it.

## 7. AUDIO / MUSIC LANGUAGE

### The Score
- **Primary texture:** Noir jazz (sparse, modal, dark) under investigation and character scenes. Never melodic in a traditional sense — more tonal and rhythmic.
- **Horror escalation:** The jazz gives way to electronic drone as the occult elements surface. The bass component of the score deepens and becomes more deliberate as the series progresses.
- **Action sequences:** Percussion-forward, irregular rhythms (not martial or heroic — chaotic and grinding).

### The Bass / Command Motif — THE KEY SIGNATURE
This is the audio equivalent of the obedience mechanism. Rules:
- A specific low-frequency bass tone is established by Ch.3 (the club bass) and recurs in every scene where the Lich's influence is present.
- This tone is **below normal music mix** — it is felt rather than heard. If possible in the export, it should be present on a subwoofer channel.
- The tone has a specific pulse interval — every 4 seconds, approximately. It never speeds up (urgency would be a human response) and never slows down. It is the patience of something ancient.
- **When a character is affected by the obedience reflex**, the bass tone gets slightly louder (by 2–3 dB) in the mix — not loud enough to be obvious on first viewing, but enough that on rewatch the viewer can confirm: the bass was always there.
- **Sinclair's sensitivity (Ch.4+):** After she's exposed, she hears the bass tone in silence. In the motel scene and the car scene, the bass tone should be audible at very low volume under the ambient sound — she is not imagining it. We can hear it too.

### Diegetic Music at The Velvet Abyss
- The club music is a heavy, slow electronic/industrial track — not dance music but something using dance music's skeleton without its joy.
- The tempo should be deliberately slow (80–90 BPM) so the bass pulses feel like heartbeats.
- When Voss seizes the comms channel, the club music continues diegetically — which means the command signal is already in the air.

### Silence as a Weapon
- The body-cam footage (Ch.1): the ambient sound drops to near-silence before the thing climbs out of the car. This silence is the scariest audio beat in Season 1.
- The mirror room (Ch.4): no music. Ambient room tone only. The shape appears in silence.
- Kane's apartment (Ch.1, discovery): the flashlight sweep is accompanied only by breath and floorboards. No score.

### Cho's Morgue (Ch.6 Prologue)
- The midnight morgue ambient is a specific sound design target: HVAC hum, distant traffic (very low), the mechanical sounds of refrigeration.
- The "whispering" is **not literal whispered words** — it is a texture: like air through old paper. A processed white noise layer with very subtle formant filtering to suggest voice-shape without intelligibility.
- When the charm falls, the whispering stops. This silence should be louder than the whispering.
