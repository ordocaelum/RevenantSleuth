# SB_S02E04_Sc01 — Cho Tests the Vial
- Source: The Story, Chapter 5 "Cold Evidence" — morgue, Cho tests the vial, impossible readings, centrifuge rejoin
- Location: INT. MORGUE / AUTOPSY SUITE — DAY
- Characters: Kane, Dr. Harlan Cho
- Beat summary: Kane brings the vial from the Velvet Abyss to Cho. The coroner runs standard chemical analysis. Every reading is impossible. The fluid resists chemical separation — when centrifuged, the separated bands reassemble within minutes. When heated, the temperature reading drops. Cho is disturbed. He locks the vial in a controlled container and tells Kane not to let it into a public lab chain.

## Blocking & Camera
This scene is about two professionals confronting evidence that violates their professional framework. Neither of them dramatizes it. Cho's discomfort is in economy of movement — the careful setting-down, the locked box, the pause before he speaks.

The centrifuge rejoin beat (shots 13–17) is the VFX centerpiece of the episode. Hold on the vial in Cho's hands — slow the edit pace here. Let the audience and Cho watch together. The bands rejoin silently. No dramatic audio sting. That silence is the horror.

## Dialogue (adapted — consistent, not exact)
> CHO: Where did this come from?

> KANE: The Velvet Abyss. Back-of-house cold storage. There's a fridge with industrial volume of them.

> CHO: *(without opening it)* It smells wrong through the cap. Not chemical-wrong. The other kind of wrong.

> KANE: What does that mean?

> CHO: It means I'm putting gloves on first.

> *(Time cut — testing sequence.)*

> CHO: The centrifuge separated it. Three bands. Clear as anything. Then —

> *(He holds the vial. They watch the bands rejoin.)*

> KANE: How long did that take?

> CHO: About ninety seconds. Faster the second time I tried.

> KANE: You tried twice.

> CHO: I wanted to make sure I wasn't wrong about being disturbed.

> CHO: The thermal reading. I added forty degrees Celsius to a measured sample. The readout went down six degrees. The sample got colder as I heated it.

> KANE: *(quietly)* It steals the heat.

> CHO: That's one way to describe active thermodynamic defiance, yes. *(beat)* Don't run this through a public lab chain. Not because of the chain of custody — because of what the people at the other end of that chain would do with something they don't understand.

> KANE: What is it?

> CHO: *(a long beat)* Something that doesn't want to be taken apart. *(puts the locked box in the drawer)* I'll tell you what I find. Come back tomorrow.
